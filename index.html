How bonus maturity works

A quick guide — no finance background needed.

The big idea

When you sell a recurring-revenue (RMR) agreement, you earn a bonus on it. Part is paid right away, and part is held back until the account proves itself. "Maturity" is the moment that held-back part is earned and ready to be paid.

Your bonus comes in two parts

Initial payout — paid right away when the agreement activates.
Deferred balance — held back, released later once the account matures.

How big each piece is depends on the contract term:

Contract termPaid upfrontHeld (deferred)
Month-to-month25%75%
12 months50%50%
24 months75%25%
36 months100%none
60 months100%none

A longer commitment pays more upfront and holds back less. 36- and 60-month deals pay the whole bonus at activation — nothing is deferred, so there's no maturity to wait on. Those show as "Paid upfront."

What "matured" actually means

The deferred balance matures the first month the account's running cash can cover it — and still stay at or above $0.

The tool tracks money in versus money out, month by month, adding it up as it goes. The moment that running total is big enough to pay you the deferred balance without going negative, the bonus is matured and becomes payable. Until then it's held; once you collect it, you mark it paid.

What speeds it up or slows it down

Money in: what the account is billed that month (a flat monthly amount, or a custom billing schedule).
Money out: monthly service cost, inspection labor, any subcontractor cost, one-time startup, and the initial bonus paid at activation.

So: a big early bill matures it fast (a prepaid account can mature in month 1). Heavy costs like a subcontractor or thin margins build cash slower, so it matures later. And a bigger deferred balance takes longer for the cash to catch up.

The renewal catch

Sometimes an account won't mature until after its initial term ends. Then releasing the deferred balance depends on the customer actually renewing. Those get an orange "renewal required" flag — earned, but conditional.

What the colors mean

Payable matured and owed to you
Maturing set to release next quarter
Held further out, or won't mature in the modeled window

The colors update based on the quarter you're viewing, so clicking through quarters shows what was due then, now, and next.

A quick example

Two identical 12-month agreements, each earning a $528 bonus ($264 upfront, $264 deferred). With standard monthly billing, the account takes about 3 months to cover the deferred balance. With a large prepaid first bill, the cash is there immediately and it matures in month 1. Same bonus — only the timing changes, based on when the money arrives.

One thing that's easy to misread

The gross margin % tells you how profitable the recurring service is — it's a health signal, not a payout rule. Margin doesn't hold back or shrink your bonus. It only affects maturity indirectly: a thin-margin account builds cash slower, so it matures a little later. Read them separately — margin is account health, maturity is when the deferred bonus is ready.
